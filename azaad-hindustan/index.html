  • Did you know:

    One of the biggest uprisings against British sarkar, this is also called as India's first war of independence. How? So Britishers got swanky new rifles for the Indian sepoys, but Indians were like WTF?! Why? Because the cartridges were made of cow and pig fat, which is a dhamaka combo of offending both Hindus and Muslims at once. Mangal Pandey, the Bengal Native Infantry's jawaan rebelled and shot a firang officer. This was fuelled by resentments against British governance and harsh land taxes, and created a widespread mutiny lasting for months. 2017 marks 160th year of the rebellion.

  • Did you know:

It was planned and funded by were Indian immigrants in USA and 'Cannedda'. The campaign also had a weekly paper 'The Ghadar', which had the tagline: Angrezi Raj Ka Dushman – Yes, Full Tadka Maarke.   • Did you know:

    Much like the Indian Premier League, this was a star-studded affair but for the Indian freedom struggle. The movement lasted from 1916 to 1918 under the leadership of Mahatma Gandhi. Joseph Baptista, Muhammad Ali Jinnah, Bal Gangadhar Tilak, Annie Besant were other prominent members. The leaders gave dhaansu speeches, and petitioned British govt. with thousands of Indians as signatories. The pressure of the movement led to the Montague's declaration on 20 August 1917 which stated that "progressive realization of responsible government in India" was the policy of the British government.

  • Did you know:

    This is one movement Indians apparently still love – to disobey the rules. Gandhi ji led this movement. It was because of the resentment to British actions such as the Rowlatt Act and Jallianwala Bagh massacre. The movement attracted a lot of masses, and saw large scale boycott of government institutions, the courts, government service, foreign goods, and elections. In 1922 a police station was set afire in Chauri Chaura. Mahatma Gandhi felt that the revolt was veering off-course and called off the non-cooperation movement. Gandhi ji was arrested after a few days.

  • Did you know:

    A few revolutionary groups weren't too excited with Gandhi ji's Civil Disobedience movement, and wanted to go 'all guns blazing', literally. The Hindustan Republican Association led by 'kraantikaaris' like Chandrashekhar Azad, Ramprasad Bismil organised armed revolution to end the colonial rule and establish a Federal Republic of the United States of India (USI? Duh!). The Kakori Train Robbery was an act by this group. Bhagat Singh, Chandrashekhar and Rajguru aligned to this movement, and actually 'had a blast' inside the Central Legislative Assembly. It's among the most loved movements of Bollywood, with a film made on almost each incident.

  • Did you know:

    Exactly 2 decades before India adopted its constitution, the Indian National Congress adopted a resolution declaring Purna Swaraj — a full and final farewell to British Raj. In 1928, the British government formed a fully-firang commission. Indians didn't find this Fair or Lovely, so they revolted saying 'Simon Go Back'. And then Gandhi ji launched the movement for 'Namak risk ka' – The Salt Satyagrah against the British salt monopoly. As a result of the denial of reforms and political rights, and the persistent ignorance of Indian political parties, the desire to oust the British completely got stronger.

  • Did you know:

    After Non-Cooperation movement and Purna Swaraj, Gandhi ji rubbed more salt on British wounds with the Salt March. To produce salt without paying the compulsory British tax, Gandhi led the March from Sabarmati Ashram, Ahmedabad to Dandi. This took 24 days. The Salt March spiced things up for the independence movement and it pinched the 'Angreji sarkaar'. And everybody wanted to have 'desh ka namak' so people joined the Civil Disobedience movement in hordes. Gandhi ji felt an item of daily use could resonate more with all classes of citizens than an abstract demand for greater political rights.

  • Did you know:

    In 1942, Mahatma Gandhi told Indians what almost every engineering aspirant is told in coaching institutes, "Karo ya Maro". The British govt was busy with World War 2 and told Indian leaders 'Sorry can't help. BRB after the war'. Not every leader in the Congress was amused with this movement. However, the British government realised that a long term rule in India, 'Beta humse na ho paayega'. Due to the cost of World War II the British Raj decided to exit gracefully and peacefully.

  • Did you know:

    Subhash Chandra Bose said, "Tum mujhe khoon do main tumhe aazaadi dunga". This is probably one of the classiest exchange offers ever given. Bose was building an army to liberate India with the help of ex-prisoners and thousands of civilian volunteers from the Indian expatriate population in Malaysia and Burma. Local civilians – with no military experience – joined the INA, which included barristers, traders and plantation workers. Violent confrontations broke out between the police and protesters all over India, in support of the INA men. Post the war courts-martial were held in public at the Red Fort in Delhi, on which the recently released Bollywood movie 'Raag Desh' is based.

  • Did you know:

    How far can you go to protest against bad food? The Royal Indian Navy started a mutiny dissatisfied with food given to them and the general conditions. The mutiny started in Bombay Dock and quickly spread to every naval establishment in the country involving more than 70 ships and 20,000 sailors. Effectively it told the Britishers to 'Hatt ja taau', and it was supported by Subash Chandra Bose's INA. Naval personnel began calling themselves the "Indian National Navy" and offered left-handed salutes to British officers. Basically, 'Swag Mera Desi' in pre-Independence era. The revolt was called off after a meeting between the President of the Naval Central Strike Committee and the Congress, following which 476 sailors were dismissed from the Royal Indian Navy.
